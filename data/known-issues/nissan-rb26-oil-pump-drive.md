# Nissan RB26DETT (BNR32/33/34 GT-R) — Oil pump drive failure and oil starvation

**English** | [日本語](./nissan-rb26-oil-pump-drive.ja.md)

## Scope

- **Vehicles:** Skyline GT-R BNR32 (1989–1994), BCNR33 (1995–1998), BNR34 (1999–2002); RB26-swapped cars inherit the issue
- **Engines/components:** RB26DETT 2.6L twin-turbo inline-six — crankshaft oil pump drive collar, oil pump inner gear, sump oil control
- **Production window:** All RB26 years; **early BNR32 cranks with the narrow (short-engagement) drive collar are the worst case** — later cranks and N1 parts widened the drive
- **Markets:** Originally JDM; now global via imports

## The issue

The RB26's oil pump is driven off a small flatted collar on the crank nose. On early cranks the engagement is narrow, and sustained high rpm — where the RB26 is happiest — hammers the pump's inner gear until the drive or gear cracks. The pump stops making pressure and the engine destroys its bearings within moments. Compounding this, the stock sump lets oil surge away from the pickup under hard cornering, so track-driven GT-Rs can starve the bearings even with a healthy pump. In the GT-R community this combination is *the* canonical RB26 killer.

## Symptoms an owner can detect

- Little warning in the classic failure: sudden oil pressure collapse (gauge drops to zero) followed by bearing knock — often at or just after sustained high rpm
- Precursors worth catching: oil pressure needle flickering or sagging in long corners; metallic glitter in drained oil or filter; faint rattle from the front of the engine at idle (damaged pump gear)
- On inspection: pulled front cover reveals cracked pump gears; oil pan debris

## Severity

**Critical.** When the drive or pump gear lets go the engine is destroyed in seconds; on current GT-R values the rebuild is a six-figure-yen-times-ten event.

## Frequency (honest assessment)

Strongly use-dependent. Street-driven, rev-disciplined cars often live long lives on the stock arrangement. Circuit and drift use at sustained 7,000 rpm+ on an unmodified early BNR32 crank is the classic recipe; specialists treat pump/collar upgrades as mandatory before track use. Later cranks are better but not immune once power and rpm rise.

## Fix and cost

- **Repair:** Preventive is the whole game: extended/wide drive collar or splined oil pump drive kit, uprated pump (N1 or aftermarket), baffled sump or extended pickup, oil restrictors and improved head drain-back for track use. After a failure: full engine rebuild, usually with crank replacement
- **Typical cost:** Preventive pump + drive + sump work JPY 20–50万円 installed at a GT-R specialist (Japan); post-failure rebuild JPY 150–300万円 at current parts prices / USD 15,000–30,000 at US specialists

## Buyer checklist

- Ask specifically: has the oil pump, drive collar, and sump been upgraded, by whom, with receipts? On any car with track history this is a pass/fail question
- Check oil and filter for metallic debris; verify stable hot-idle oil pressure and no front-of-engine rattle
- Treat "original unopened engine, occasional circuit use" as a rebuild-pending price, not a bonus
- Engine number and crank history matter — an early R32 crank in any chassis carries the narrow-collar risk

## Sources

Three decades of GT-R owner community and specialist shop consensus (Japan, Australia, US); Nissan's own N1/Nismo parts catalog changes (wider drive, uprated pump) corroborate the weak point. Costs reflect the current collector-era parts market — verify quotes.
