# Subaru EJ turbo (GDB WRX STI etc.) — Piston ringland cracking and rod bearing failure

**English** | [日本語](./subaru-ej20t-ringland-bearing.ja.md)

## Scope

- **Vehicles:** Impreza WRX/STI — GC8 (1992–2000), GDA/GDB (2000–2007), GRB/GVB (2007–2014); Legacy/Forester turbos of the same era. US-market WRX/STI with EJ255/EJ257 (2002–2014) are the most failure-prone variants
- **Engines/components:** EJ205/EJ207 2.0L and EJ255/EJ257 2.5L turbo flat-fours — piston ringlands, rod (big-end) bearings, oil pickup
- **Production window:** Entire EJ turbo era; risk scales with tune state and how the car has been driven rather than a specific defect window
- **Markets:** Global; JDM EJ207 is measurably tougher than the US EJ257 but not immune

## The issue

Two related failure modes define used EJ turbo ownership. First, **ringland cracking**: detonation (from poor fuel, heat soak, aggressive or careless tuning, or the lean factory-calibration edge on EJ257) cracks the thin land between piston ring grooves, causing sudden compression loss and heavy blow-by — cylinder 4 is the classic victim. Second, **rod bearing failure**: oil starvation under sustained cornering load (track use with a stock pickup/baffling) or degraded oil spins a big-end bearing. Both mean the engine comes apart; on a tuned car they often arrive without warning.

## Symptoms an owner can detect

- Ringland: sudden misfire and power loss, single low cylinder on a compression test (often #4), heavy blow-by (remove the oil filler cap at idle — strong pulsing/smoke), rapidly rising oil consumption
- Bearing: metallic knock rising with rpm, especially when warm; oil pressure light flickering in hard corners; **glitter in the drained oil or filter**
- Precursors visible on a scan/logging tool: fine knock correction being pulled repeatedly under boost, lean AFR spikes at high load

## Severity

**Major.** Failures are sudden and total for the engine, though rarely a safety event; the rebuild is a large fraction of the car's value for non-collector examples.

## Frequency (honest assessment)

Strongly condition-dependent. Bone-stock, well-maintained JDM EJ207 cars driven on good fuel routinely pass 150,000 km without drama. Risk stacks fast with: stage-tuned engines (especially quick e-tunes on EJ257), poor-quality fuel, track use on stock oiling, and unknown history. Among modified US-market EJ257s, ringland failure is common enough to be a meme; treat any tuned example as pre-failure until proven otherwise.

## Fix and cost

- **Repair:** Engine rebuild with forged pistons (and often a shortblock exchange); bearing failures usually also need a crank. Preventive: conservative professional tune with knock margin, quality fuel only, frequent quality oil changes, baffled sump/uprated pickup before track use
- **Typical cost:** JPY 50–100万円 for a rebuild at a Japanese tuner/specialist; USD 5,000–9,000 for a quality forged rebuild in the US; a new OEM-style shortblock swap somewhat less but retains stock-piston limits

## Buyer checklist

- Compression test all four cylinders (and ideally a leak-down) as a purchase condition — walk away or reprice on one low cylinder
- Listen for knock at warm idle and under a gentle load pull; check the drained oil/filter for glitter if a change is possible
- Idle blow-by check at the oil filler cap
- Demand tune history: who tuned it, on what fuel, with what logs. An unknown "stage 2" car is priced as needing a rebuild
- Verify oil change records and look for track-day evidence (pads, harness holes, tow hooks) without matching oiling upgrades

## Sources

Two decades of WRX/STI owner community documentation (Japan, US, Australia), Subaru specialist and tuner shop consensus, and US class-action/TSB history around EJ255/EJ257 short blocks. Individual risk varies enormously with history — inspect, don't assume.
