# Mazda FD3S RX-7 (13B-REW) — Rotary compression loss

**English** | [日本語](./mazda-fd3s-13b-rew-compression-loss.ja.md)

## Scope

- **Vehicles:** Mazda RX-7 FD3S (1991–2002 JDM; 1993–1995 US market as the FD). The FC3S 13B-T and RX-8 13B-MSP fail differently — this entry is 13B-REW specific
- **Engines/components:** 13B-REW twin-rotor sequential twin-turbo — apex seals, side seals, coolant (water jacket) O-ring seals
- **Production window:** All production years; this is a wear/heat life-limit issue rather than a defect window
- **Markets:** Global

## The issue

The 13B-REW runs hot in a tight engine bay, and rotary compression depends entirely on small sprung seals rather than piston rings. Heat, carbon, detonation, and age wear the apex/side seals until compression drops; overheating events also let go the internal coolant O-rings, putting coolant into the combustion chambers. Either way the outcome is the same: the engine needs a rebuild. On a 25–35-year-old chassis, the degraded vacuum-hose "rat's nest" controlling the sequential turbos adds boost-control faults that can kill an otherwise healthy engine via detonation.

## Symptoms an owner can detect

- Hard or failed **hot restart** (starts fine cold, cranks long or floods when heat-soaked) — the classic low-compression tell
- Weak, lumpy idle; loss of top-end power; falling fuel economy
- Coolant-seal failure: white sweet-smelling exhaust steam, unexplained coolant loss, bubbles in the coolant reservoir with the engine running, pressurized cooling system
- Rotary-specific compression test (three pulse readings per rotor) below ~6.5 kgf/cm² or uneven between faces/rotors
- Boost gauge showing spikes, sequential transition stumble around 4,500 rpm, or failure to hit second-turbo boost — precursors, not compression loss itself, but the usual killer

## Severity

**Major.** Failure is rarely dangerous, but the only real fix is an engine rebuild, and on today's FD3S values that job is a core part of the ownership cost.

## Frequency (honest assessment)

Not "if" but "when": essentially every 13B-REW needs seals eventually. Stock, well-cooled, sympathetically driven engines commonly reach 100,000–150,000 km between rebuilds; tuned engines, overheated engines, or cars with neglected boost-control plumbing can fail far earlier. Many surviving cars have already been rebuilt at least once — that is normal, not a red flag.

## Fix and cost

- **Repair:** Engine removal and rebuild with new apex/side/coolant seals (and often rotor housings if chrome is worn); refresh the vacuum hoses, cooling system, and ignition while the engine is out. Preventive: premix or reliable oil-metering-pump maintenance, aggressive cooling upgrades, never ignore an overheat
- **Typical cost:** JPY 60–120万円 for a quality rebuild at a rotary specialist in Japan (parts availability now inflates this); USD 6,000–12,000+ at US rotary shops. A used replacement engine is a lottery — budget as if it will also need seals

## Buyer checklist

- Insist on a **rotary compression test** (rotary-specific tester, e.g. reading all three faces per rotor) — a standard piston-engine tester's single number is not sufficient. No test, price the car as needing a rebuild
- Test hot restart yourself: drive it to full temperature, shut it off for 10–15 minutes, restart
- Check the coolant reservoir for bubbles/hydrocarbon smell with the engine idling warm
- Ask for rebuild history (who, when, what parts) — a documented specialist rebuild is a plus; "rebuilt by previous owner, no records" is not
- Look at the vacuum hoses and ask when boost control was last sorted; verify sequential transition on the test drive
